# Public Narrative

**Status:** Implemented. Phase 12 design proposal, refined by Phase 13 content contracts; this narrative is live across the public site.

The public story is designed by time and understanding, not by page count. It should reward both a five-second scan and a ten-minute investigation without forcing either visitor into the other's pace.

## Narrative promise

HubZero builds products and engineering systems, applies that practice to real client problems, publishes what it learns, and turns repeated patterns into reusable foundations.

That sentence is a strategy, not proposed hero copy. Public copy should be shorter and should prove each clause through the four pillars and their relationships.

## Understanding over time

### After 5 seconds

The visitor should understand:

- HubZero is an engineering company.
- It builds products and serious software.
- The experience is precise, current, and trustworthy.

Required signals:

- One direct identity statement.
- A real product or engineering artifact, not an abstract decoration.
- Immediate visibility of the four pillars in navigation or the opening composition.

Do not introduce team size, a service menu, process steps, or a manifesto in this window.

### After 30 seconds

The visitor should understand:

- Products are the long-term identity.
- Work, Builds, Blueprints, and Labs are distinct but connected.
- The company shows evidence and current state rather than making broad claims.
- There is a path appropriate to their intent.

Required signals:

- A featured Build with a clear product thesis and current state.
- A compact explanation of the four pillars.
- One client outcome or deployed artifact.
- One current Lab or recent Note showing activity.

### After 2 minutes

The visitor should understand:

- How an idea can move through research, a Lab, a Build, client application, and a Blueprint.
- What kind of technical decisions HubZero documents.
- Which people are accountable for the work.
- Services are real but supported by evidence.

Required signals:

- At least one traversable relationship chain.
- Architecture, decision, or outcome detail—not just summaries.
- A visible link to an Engineering Profile or attributed Note.
- A capability statement attached to Work/Build/Blueprint/Lab evidence.

### After 10 minutes

The visitor should understand:

- HubZero has a coherent engineering worldview, not a collection of aesthetic projects.
- The body of work has lineage, lessons, current investigations, and named contributors.
- What to explore next, use directly, follow over time, or discuss with HubZero.

Required signals:

- Deep Documents with meaningful structure.
- Cross-collection navigation that explains why each connection exists.
- Live deployments, repositories, technical media, references, or sourced metrics where real.
- A specific conversion or continuation path that preserves the context already gathered.

## Homepage story

The homepage is a composed editorial front. It is not a complete digest of every route.

```text
01  Identity
    “What kind of company is this?”
        ↓
02  Product signal
    “What has it made that is alive now?”
        ↓
03  Operating system
    “How do the four pillars relate?”
        ↓
04  Evidence
    “Can it solve real problems and explain the work?”
        ↓
05  Current engineering
    “What is being investigated or written now?”
        ↓
06  People and principles
    “Who is accountable, and how do they think?”
        ↓
07  Relevant engagement
    “What can I inspect, use, or discuss next?”
```

### Chapter 1 — Identity

**Job:** establish HubZero as an engineering company.

Use one short statement and one supporting sentence. The statement must still be true if a service line changes. Avoid “we help,” “digital experiences,” “innovation,” “end-to-end,” and other language that could belong to any agency.

The visual counterpart is one real Build, Lab artifact, or system state. It should not be a fabricated product collage.

### Chapter 2 — Product signal

**Job:** make the long-term identity visible immediately.

Lead with one featured Build, including what it does, current deployment state, one technical fact, and direct paths to its detail and live surface. If no Build meets the content standard, use a current Lab honestly; do not manufacture product maturity.

### Chapter 3 — The operating system

**Job:** explain the four pillars and lifecycle without turning it into diagram decoration.

Use linked prose plus a compact structural index:

- Labs investigate.
- Builds ship products.
- Work applies engineering to client problems.
- Blueprints generalize proven patterns.

The lifecycle is not always linear. Relationships show actual movement; the copy describes the recurring model without pretending every entry follows every stage.

### Chapter 4 — Evidence

**Job:** prove range without becoming a portfolio grid.

Compose three different evidence forms:

1. A Work outcome with the constraint and decision that produced it.
2. A Blueprint or Build artifact visitors can inspect directly.
3. A technical excerpt—architecture, code, metric with source, or decision record.

Each evidence item has a different visual rhythm because each answers a different question.

### Chapter 5 — Current engineering

**Job:** show the company is active.

Pair one Lab state with one recent Note. Use explicit dates and status. A stale “currently building” claim damages trust; homepage current-state material must have a recency policy and fall back to the latest genuinely meaningful update.

### Chapter 6 — People and principles

**Job:** establish accountable engineering judgment.

Introduce the team briefly, then feature one earned Engineering Profile or attributed Note. Do not turn the homepage into a team grid. Principles appear through a decision or quote tied to evidence, not as unsupported values cards.

### Chapter 7 — Relevant engagement

**Job:** offer the next action without collapsing the experience into lead generation.

Present three plain paths:

- Inspect a product or blueprint.
- Follow current engineering through Labs and Notes.
- Start a project conversation with context.

The final Contact action states what information helps and when HubZero responds.

## Collection narratives

Every collection has a distinct editorial job and therefore a distinct narrative order.

### Work — consequence and judgment

```text
Context → constraint → role → decisions → implementation → outcome → lessons → lineage
```

Lead with the problem and consequence, not a full-bleed image. Use sourced metrics only. “Challenges” should expose real constraints; “lessons” should include what changed in HubZero's practice.

### Builds — product and system

```text
Product thesis → current state → use → architecture → decisions → evidence → roadmap → lineage
```

The case-study Document carries the product story. The technical Document carries architecture and decisions. They should feel like two lenses on one product, not two unrelated articles.

### Blueprints — fit before features

```text
Intended audience → architecture (X) → design language (Y) → live preview → features → evidence → adoption path
```

Visitors must be able to disqualify a Blueprint quickly. Clear fit increases trust more than broad applicability claims.

### Labs — present tense

```text
Stage → objective → current milestone → approach → experiments/findings → blockers → graduation criteria → next update
```

Labs are not unfinished case studies. Dates, milestones, and honest blockers matter. The current Studio model separates long-form evidence into `overview`, `engineeringJournal`, `findings`, and `researchNotes` Documents; the public narrative selects only populated roles rather than rendering empty tabs. When a Lab graduates, that engineering record remains historical evidence and the transition points to the Build. A reviewed legacy `journal` may map into the engineering-journal role, but it is never duplicated across all four roles.

### Notes — one useful idea

```text
Question/claim → context → reasoning/evidence → implication → referenced work → author
```

Notes are not a company-news blog. Each Note should add a technical idea, decision record, research finding, or lesson to the public body of knowledge.

### Engineering Profiles — earned identity

```text
Overview → engineering identity → current exploration → evidence → selected long-form lenses
```

The five Document roles are available, not mandatory chapters to render blindly:

- **Introduction** establishes the engineer's context.
- **Interview** reveals judgment through specific questions and answers.
- **Timeline** shows change over time only when milestones are meaningful.
- **Quotes** surfaces concise principles backed by the surrounding profile.
- **Achievements** records verifiable outcomes, not self-promotional awards language.

The public template selects and orders populated roles deliberately. Empty roles never create empty navigation.

### About — company and roster

```text
Company thesis → how the team is organized → roster stage → principles in practice → Engineering Profiles → Contact
```

About answers who HubZero is. Profiles answer how individual engineers think. They must link to each other without duplicating biography content.

### Services — evidence by need

```text
Visitor need → service definition → proven evidence → boundaries → conversation
```

Do not repeat pillar summaries or invent process diagrams. A service with no evidence links remains draft.

## Editorial cross-linking

Cross-links are part of the story, not post-body recommendations.

- A Lab Note can appear next to the exact milestone or finding it explains.
- A Build architecture decision can link back to the Lab experiment that produced it.
- Work can show which Build or Blueprint informed the implementation.
- A Blueprint can show the Work and Builds that proved its pattern.
- A Profile can foreground current exploration, authored Notes, and selected evidence across all pillars.
- A Note's byline should resolve to a public Profile when one exists, otherwise to the public Team identity without fabricating a Profile.

Use no more than one primary relationship cluster at a time. A dense graph is a data visualization problem, not good default navigation.

## Voice and copy rules

- Use direct nouns and verbs.
- Prefer technical specificity over technical theater.
- Explain consequences when a term may be unfamiliar; do not flatten correct engineering vocabulary.
- Avoid superlatives, category clichés, and claims of being “engineering-first.” Demonstrate it.
- Write status plainly: “Testing,” “Last major update 14 July 2026,” “Repository private,” “Deployment retired.”
- Distinguish current fact, interpretation, and future intent.
- Never fabricate clients, metrics, testimonials, contributors, repositories, or release maturity.

### Headline test

A strong headline names one of:

- the thing built;
- the constraint resolved;
- the decision made;
- the change produced;
- the question investigated.

If it only expresses ambition, it belongs in draft.

## Content completeness gates

| Type | Minimum public evidence |
|---|---|
| Work | Clear problem, HubZero role, at least one decision, real outcome or honest qualitative result, lessons |
| Build | Product thesis, current state, architecture overview, technical decision, media or live artifact |
| Blueprint | Valid name, intended audience, X/Y explanation, live deployment, features, technologies |
| Lab | Stage, objective, current milestone, dated journal evidence, next direction or blocker |
| Note | Specific thesis/question, substantive body, author, publication date, at least one useful reference when claims require it |
| Engineering Profile | Public Team member, overview, engineering identity, current exploration, at least two meaningful evidence links, one substantive Document role |
| Service | Specific definition, at least two strong evidence links where the collection permits it, clear boundary |

These are editorial gates for future implementation and seeding, not new schema validation in Phase 12.

## Narrative failure modes

- Leading with a service catalog before products or evidence.
- Giving all four pillars equal homepage space regardless of content strength.
- Presenting Labs as polished products or Builds as marketing landing pages.
- Using Notes as announcements without technical value.
- Rendering every relationship as an undifferentiated card carousel.
- Repeating biographies across About and Engineering Profiles.
- Allowing motion to determine when content becomes readable.
- Using empty scale and whitespace as a substitute for editorial hierarchy.
