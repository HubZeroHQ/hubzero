# 00 — AI Design Guide

> **Status: Canonical — 2026-07-04.** This is the entry point for every design-related task at HubZero, human-directed or AI-directed. It does not supersede any `ARCHITECTURE/` document; it sits beside them as the design counterpart to `ARCHITECTURE/01_PRODUCT_VISION.md` and `02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md`, and it codifies decisions already made in `07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md`, `15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md`, and `17_COMPANY_STRUCTURE.md`, plus the concrete lessons of `docs/design-reviews/MARKETING_SITE_REVIEW_V1.md` — the first time HubZero's own design principles were checked against a real, shipped site and found to be followed in one place and abandoned three pages later.
>
> **Read this before opening a design tool, writing a design prompt, or reviewing a design output — AI or human.** If a task involves a page, a component's visual treatment, a prompt to an image generator, or a design conversation in any tool (Claude Code, ChatGPT, Midjourney, Figma AI, a code review of visual work), this document governs it.

## 0. What this document is not

This is not a style guide. A style guide tells you which hex code to use. `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` does that.

This is not a component library. A component library tells you what a `Button` looks like and what props it takes. HubZero's actual components live in `src/components`.

This is not a branding document. A branding document owns voice, naming, and positioning. `ARCHITECTURE/02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` does that, and this document defers to it on every question of what HubZero says about itself.

**This document teaches judgment.** Tokens, components, and voice rules tell you what HubZero has already decided. This document tells you _how to decide_ the things those other documents don't cover — which is most of what a new page, a new prompt, or a new design review actually has to figure out. A design system can tell you the H1 is 40–48px; it cannot tell you whether a given page should open with a headline at all, or what a visitor should feel three seconds after the page loads. That judgment is what this document exists to transfer.

## 1. Purpose

HubZero has produced two kinds of design work so far. The homepage (`ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md`, two founder-approval rounds, an explicit uniqueness test applied beat-by-beat) is the first kind: deliberate, argued, checked against a standard before it shipped. The Services, Software Engineering, and Hardware & Embedded pages, built immediately after, are the second kind: competent, on-brand, individually well-executed — and, per the commissioned review that finally looked at all seven pages as one journey, "the same template wearing different copy" (`docs/design-reviews/MARKETING_SITE_REVIEW_V1.md` §4). Same caption format, same headline treatment, same mono facts line, same numbered-panel-over-texture block, same italic pull-quote, same border-top CTA close, in the same order, three times in a row. No single page in that set violates a written rule. The rules simply weren't consulted a second time.

That is the exact failure this document exists to prevent, and it is worth being specific about why it happened, because the reason is not "the second designer was worse than the first." The reason is that the reasoning behind the homepage's decisions — why the serif appears only twice, why the whitespace pause before the case-study image is rare rather than routine, why the CTA close mirrors the hero instead of using a generic banner — lived in a document scoped to the homepage. Nothing forced the next three pages to re-derive that reasoning from first principles instead of copying the homepage's _output_. A template is what you get when good decisions get treated as a formula instead of an argument that has to be won again for the next page.

**Why HubZero intentionally rejects generic AI-generated design.** Every image generator, every code-generation model, and every "modern SaaS landing page" prompt converges on the same visual vocabulary, because that vocabulary is the statistical center of what's been generated ten million times before: a hero with a gradient headline, a three-card feature grid, a glassmorphic panel, a stat row with round numbers, a testimonial carousel. None of that is _wrong_ in the sense of being broken — it renders, it's responsive, it passes a component audit. It is wrong for HubZero specifically because HubZero's entire differentiation strategy (`ARCHITECTURE/02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §5–6) is that trust is built through specificity and restraint, not through visual conventions borrowed from companies with a different problem to solve. A generic AI-generated site is, definitionally, a site that could belong to anyone. HubZero's actual competitive claim — small, founder-led, combined software and hardware engineering, accountable after delivery — has no generic visual expression. If the design looks like every other AI-assisted output, the design has quietly contradicted the copy standing right next to it.

**Why consistency matters more than novelty.** This document is not a mandate for constant reinvention. `ARCHITECTURE/02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §5 (research principle 7) is explicit that visual identity should be "consistent rather than novel per-page" — the same type scale, the same color tokens, the same spacing rhythm, everywhere. What must vary per page is _composition_: the macro-arrangement of those consistent parts into a structure that matches what that specific page is trying to prove. `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §8 draws this line precisely: "A shared _component_ is correct engineering and does not fail [the uniqueness test] on its own. A shared _composition_ — the same macro-structure, image placement, and beat order reused across multiple pages — does fail it, even if every individual component is well-built." Reusing a button component ten times is engineering discipline. Reusing a page's entire shape ten times is the thing that makes a visitor stop reading. This document's job is to keep those two categories from getting confused, because that exact confusion is what produced the Services/Software/Hardware pattern.

## 2. Project philosophy

These nine principles are not a mood board. Each one is a decision rule that resolves a real disagreement a designer — human or AI — will otherwise have to guess at mid-task.

**Editorial instead of agency.** An agency site is organized around convincing you the company is capable: services list, process diagram, team grid, testimonial wall — proof by assertion and repetition. An editorial page is organized the way a well-edited magazine feature is: it has a point of view, it earns your attention beat by beat, and it trusts a reader who's still there at paragraph four to have been convinced by the argument, not by a stat badge. `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` names this distinction directly — Round 1 of the homepage was rejected specifically for being "an agency site model... wearing an editorial skin." The tell is structural: an agency page is built from swappable sections (a services block, a stats block, a testimonial block) that could be reordered without damage. An editorial page is built from beats that hand off to each other with intent — each one exists because the previous one raised a question the next one answers (`ARCHITECTURE/15` §2's "narrative arc," not "information hierarchy"). If a section could be deleted, reordered, or duplicated with no loss of meaning, it was built as an agency block, not an editorial beat.

**Engineering-first instead of marketing-first.** HubZero's actual asset is that the people who scope a project are the people who build it (`ARCHITECTURE/01_PRODUCT_VISION.md` §4). A marketing-first design instinct reaches for language and imagery designed to _persuade_ — bold claims, aspirational photography, a confident illustration of "success." An engineering-first instinct reaches for language and imagery that _demonstrates_ — a real stack list, a real metric measured against a real production build, a real system diagram. The Labs/R&D data-flow diagram on the Hardware page is the model case: it is not decoration, it is not a stock illustration standing in for competence, it is an actual technical artifact rendered at the type system's scale (`docs/design-reviews/MARKETING_SITE_REVIEW_V1.md` §5 calls it "the single most 'this team actually thinks like engineers' moment on the site outside the case study itself"). When a design decision has to choose between looking impressive and being verifiably true, verifiably true wins, every time, because verifiably true is the only kind of impressive a skeptical CTO evaluating a six-figure engagement actually trusts.

**Typography before decoration.** `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` §1 states this as a working rule for the homepage: "Typography is the primary visual identity of this page — not the circuit motif, not color. Every other decision in this document should be checked against whether it's competing with type for that role." Generalize it: on any HubZero page, if you removed every decorative element — texture, motif, gradient, image — and were left with only headline, subhead, and body copy set at the correct scale and rhythm, the page should still read as confident and well-composed. If a page only _works_ once the texture and motif are added, the composition was weak and the decoration is compensating for it, which is precisely the "thin content wearing effects" failure `02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §5 (research principle 2) warns against.

**Premium through restraint.** The research synthesis behind HubZero's brand strategy is blunt about this: "the premium feeling does not come from any single visual trick — it comes from the _absence_ of things amateur sites do" (`ARCHITECTURE/02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §5). A limited palette, one accent color used with intent, generous whitespace, motion that's brief and purposeful — these all signal the same thing: _we don't need to convince you with effects._ This is also, not incidentally, a claim a four-to-five-person studio can make as credibly as a funded company, because restraint costs discipline, not budget. Every time a design adds a second accent color, a second novelty typeface, a second decorative motif "because it looks nice," it is spending down that credibility signal for a marginal visual gain — and the research is explicit that visitors read the _absence_ of that behavior as confidence, not as a missed opportunity.

**Motion with purpose.** `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §4 states the rule for engineering: motion should explain a mechanism, never decorate. This document extends it to a design-judgment test, not just an engineering one: before any motion is specified — in a prompt, in a design spec, in a review — name the specific thing a viewer understands _because_ of the motion that they would not understand from a static frame. "It feels more alive" is not an answer to that question; "it shows the reader that this number is a real, live count of something, not a static claim" is. If no answer exists, the motion is decoration wearing motion's clothes (`ARCHITECTURE/15` §8's own phrase for this exact failure mode), and the honest fix is to delete it, not to soften it.

**Every page earns its own composition.** This is the direct, generalized lesson of the Services/Software/Hardware failure (§1 above, `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §8). A page composition is not a container that content gets poured into — it is an argument, in visual form, for why this particular page's content deserves to be read in this particular order and shape. Two pages making structurally similar claims (Software Engineering and Hardware & Embedded both say "here's what we do, here's proof, here's how an engagement runs") are still allowed — required, even — to make that claim in genuinely different visual shapes, the same way two magazine features on related topics still get different layouts from the same art director. Sameness of _content category_ is not license for sameness of _composition_. See §6 below for how this applies pillar by pillar.

**Design should improve comprehension.** Every visual decision is answerable to one question: does this make it easier or harder for the reader to understand what HubZero is telling them? A texture that visually separates two service panels without needing a label improves comprehension (a reader can tell the panels are different practice areas at a glance). A texture that's present because the section "felt empty" without it does not — it's solving a design anxiety, not a reader's problem. This is also the test that resolves most typography, color, and layout disputes without needing a separate rule for each: hierarchy exists so a reader's eye goes where the meaning is, not so the page looks "designed."

**Never fabricate credibility.** This principle is inherited directly and without modification from `ARCHITECTURE/05_CONTENT_STRATEGY.md`'s zero-fabrication policy and `01_PRODUCT_VISION.md` §9's explicit ban on inflated numbers and placeholder testimonials — and it is a _design_ principle as much as a content one, because design is exactly where fabricated credibility hides. A stat badge with a suspiciously round number, a "trusted by" logo wall with no real client names attached, a testimonial-style pull-quote formatted to look like a real quote when it's actually marketing copy the design team wrote — these are visual lies even when every word on the page is technically true. `ARCHITECTURE/17_COMPANY_STRUCTURE.md` §6 extends this same discipline to Labs and Builds specifically _because_ no client is watching those pages to catch an exaggeration — the absence of an external check is exactly when this principle matters most, not when it can relax.

**Every decision should support trust.** This is the principle every other one in this section ultimately serves, and the operative test comes straight from `ARCHITECTURE/02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §2's copy rule, generalized to visuals: **could a competitor's website look like this and be equally credible?** If a composition, a motif, or an image treatment would work identically well for a company HubZero has never heard of, it isn't doing trust-building work — it's neutral at best, and at worst it's borrowed credibility from a visual language (glossy SaaS, agency-generic) that actively undercuts HubZero's specific claim to be a small, real, accountable engineering team rather than a company performing bigness it doesn't have.

## 3. Things AI must never produce

Every item below fails one or more of the principles in §2. They're grouped by failure mode, not alphabetized, because the grouping is itself the lesson: these are not forty unrelated rules to memorize, they are a small number of underlying mistakes wearing forty different costumes.

### 3.1 Layout clichés (fail "editorial instead of agency" and "every page earns its own composition")

- **SaaS hero layouts** — centered headline, centered subhead, two centered buttons, a product screenshot in a browser-chrome mockup floating below. This is the single most statistically common hero on the internet precisely because it's a safe default for a self-serve software product with no story to tell. HubZero is not self-serve and has a story to tell; using the layout designed to compensate for that absence contradicts the site's own premise.
- **Symmetric three/four-card feature grids** ("Fast," "Secure," "Reliable," each with a small icon) used as a page's primary explanatory device. This pattern explains nothing — it asserts three adjectives with no evidence behind any of them, which is the exact "icon-grid-with-vague-claims" pattern `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §7 explicitly excludes from the v2 design system.
- **A generic two-or-three-card grid for "What We Do."** `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` §1 names this exclusion directly for the homepage; it generalizes to every page describing HubZero's capabilities. Unequal, differently-textured panels (the actual shipped pattern) communicate that the two disciplines are genuinely distinct; a matching card grid communicates the opposite.
- **Template repetition across a page family.** The Services/Software/Hardware failure, named in §1, is the canonical example: identical beat order, identical component set, identical transitions, different words. This is the single highest-priority item on this list because it is the one HubZero has already produced in production, not a hypothetical.
- **The border-top CTA-close block reused as the default page ending**, past the two or three pages where it's actually earning its place. Consistency in a _recurring, intentional_ device (§6's cross-references) is fine; consistency because no one considered an alternative is the same failure as template repetition, just isolated to one beat instead of a whole page.

### 3.2 Visual-effects clichés (fail "premium through restraint" and "typography before decoration")

- **Purple-to-indigo gradients**, or any gradient used as a default background treatment. HubZero has exactly one approved gradient token (`--brand-gradient`, blue→violet→gold), used in one or two places per page maximum, never as a section background (`ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §2). A generic purple/indigo SaaS gradient is not even that token — it's the specific visual signature of a completely different category of company.
- **Glassmorphism as a default surface treatment.** A `GlassCard`-style blurred panel exists in HubZero's base component vocabulary and is permitted "sparingly" (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §5) — but reaching for it as the default way to present a block of content is the failure mode, not the component's mere existence. Ask whether the blur is doing a specific job (visually separating overlapping layers) before using it at all.
- **Floating cards with heavy drop shadows**, especially stacked or rotated for "dynamism." This is a stock visual signature of consumer-app marketing sites and has no relationship to HubZero's engineering-first, editorial identity.
- **Decorative blur orbs** behind sections. Explicitly removed from the v2 design system as a default pattern (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §2) after being identified as the legacy site's single most overused device (8+ components). Permitted, if at all, once per page, at low opacity — never as filler for a section that feels visually empty, because a section that feels empty needs stronger content or more disciplined whitespace, not a background effect masking the gap.
- **Excessive animation** — anything beyond one orchestrated load-in sequence plus brief, purposeful one-shot reveals on scroll (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §4). Parallax for its own sake, scroll-jacking, autoplay decorative loops, and "make it feel alive" wrapper animations on every element are all explicitly excluded. The test from `ARCHITECTURE/15` §8 is the operative one: if asked "what animated on this page?", a reader who was actually reading should honestly answer "I'm not sure."
- **Decorative complexity and visual noise** generally — competing textures, multiple motifs active in the same viewport, gradient text next to a gradient border next to a gradient button. HubZero permits exactly one signature motif (the circuit-trace pattern) and restricts even that to two appearances per page maximum where it's already in use (`ARCHITECTURE/15` §1). A second motif, however tasteful in isolation, dilutes the first.

### 3.3 Content and copy clichés (fail "never fabricate credibility" and "engineering-first instead of marketing-first")

- **Empty statistics** — round, unverifiable, or unsourced numbers ("100% satisfaction," "24/7 availability," "~12 teammates") used to imply scale or reliability with nothing behind them. Explicitly named as a legacy failure and banned outright (`ARCHITECTURE/01_PRODUCT_VISION.md` §9).
- **Fake or unattributed testimonials**, including placeholder names ("Sarah Johnson, Tech Entrepreneur") standing in for real clients who haven't been asked yet. Remove rather than fabricate — an honest gap reads better than a fictional endorsement, and it always eventually gets checked (`01_PRODUCT_VISION.md` §9).
- **Generic startup illustrations** — people at whiteboards, rockets, puzzle pieces, abstract blob shapes standing in for "innovation" or "collaboration." Explicitly excluded (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §7): no stock photography, no abstract hero illustrations.
- **Decorative icons without meaning** — an icon attached to a claim not because it clarifies the claim but because the section "needed something visual." If a reader could swap the icon for a different one without changing the claim's meaning, the icon wasn't doing work.
- **Marketing clichés in copy that leak into visual design** — a headline like "Bring your ideas to life" paired with a hero visual built to match that vagueness (soft gradients, abstract shapes) rather than a specific claim. `ARCHITECTURE/02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §2's competitor-sentence test applies to visuals exactly as it does to copy: could a generic agency's hero look identical to this and be equally true of them? If yes, redesign it.
- **Trust-badge walls and countdown/urgency banners.** Both are explicitly excluded from the v2 system (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §7) — they belong to a persuasion model (borrowed authority, artificial scarcity) that contradicts HubZero's evidence-based trust strategy (§2 above, "never fabricate credibility").
- **Testimonial carousels.** Removed along with the legacy content it displayed (`06_PAGE_SPECIFICATIONS.md`, referenced in `07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §7). A carousel is also, mechanically, a device for hiding weak or thin content behind motion — exactly the failure mode §2's "motion with purpose" principle exists to prevent.

### 3.4 Overuse of HubZero's own devices — the second-order trap

This category is the newest and most important addition to this list, because it's the one the marketing review actually caught in production, and it's a subtler failure than copying a generic template: **taking a real, approved HubZero device and using it so often it stops meaning anything.**

- **The italic serif pull-quote (Instrument Serif), used as a routine section-opener rather than a rare slow-down moment.** `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` §1's Round 2 rule is explicit: "the serif appears at a small, named set of moments where the reader should genuinely slow down — not once per beat automatically." The shipped site used it six times across seven pages (`docs/design-reviews/MARKETING_SITE_REVIEW_V1.md` §4) — "it stops reading as 'the one slow-down moment' and starts reading as 'the app's H3 treatment.'" A device that is _supposed_ to be rare and becomes common has silently changed category, from "a moment" to "a style," without anyone deciding that on purpose.
- **The "unexpected whitespace pause before a full-bleed image" technique, applied as a general habit instead of a single deliberate climax.** Scoped in `ARCHITECTURE/15` §8a as _one_ memorable moment on the homepage; found reused before nearly every image block across the shipped site (§4 of the review). The fix named in the review is the right general rule: reserve a rare technique for one or two true climax moments per page family, and let every other image transition use the standard section rhythm.
- **A process/step list ("Discovery → Build → Stay," worded differently each time) repeated on three or more pages with no new information added each time.** The review found this exact pattern on Home, Software, and Hardware and noted "by the third occurrence, a reader already knows the shape of the answer before reading it." Repetition of a true claim is not free — each repetition without new evidence spends down the reader's attention for zero additional trust.
- **General rule this category implies:** before reusing any HubZero-specific device — the circuit motif, the serif pull-quote, the mono facts line, the whitespace-pause, a specific process framing — check how many times it already appears across the pages a reader is likely to see in one session. A device budgeted for "rare" that's about to become its third or fourth use in one journey needs either a genuinely new job to do, or to be left out this time.

## 4. Approved design language

This section explains the _principles_ behind HubZero's design language. Exact numeric tokens (type scale, spacing values, color hex/OKLCH values) are owned by `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` and should be read directly from there — they are not duplicated here because a token document and a principles document drift apart the moment a number changes in only one of them.

**Typography.** One typeface family, used with total discipline (Geist Sans for the great majority of copy, Geist Mono reserved for genuinely technical content, one display serif reserved for genuinely rare slow-down moments). The discipline is the point, not the specific typefaces — if HubZero's type family ever changes, this rule survives unchanged: pick as few families as the content genuinely needs distinct voices for, and defend every one of them. A typeface earns a role by doing a job no other typeface on the page does (Mono signals "this is a verifiable fact"; Serif signals "slow down, this sentence matters"); a typeface used because it "looks nice" hasn't earned a role and shouldn't be on the page.

**Whitespace.** Whitespace is not empty space left over after content is placed — it's an active design decision that HubZero's own research explicitly names ("spend money on emptiness," `02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §5). Two distinct uses of whitespace exist and should not be confused: _rhythm_ whitespace (the consistent vertical spacing between ordinary beats, which should feel steady and unremarkable) and _emphasis_ whitespace (a deliberately larger pause used to signal "what comes next matters more than what came before," which only works because it's rare — see §3.4). Using emphasis-level whitespace at rhythm frequency is the exact failure the review caught; it's covered here as a positive design-language rule, not just a prohibition, because the fix isn't "use less whitespace," it's "reserve the large gap for the moment that's actually earned it and tighten the rest to standard rhythm."

**Rhythm and hierarchy.** A page has rhythm when a reader's scroll speed and attention naturally track the content's actual importance — they linger on the case study, they move quickly through a compact process list, without being told to. This is achieved through the interaction of type scale, spacing, and beat _height_ being proportional to narrative weight rather than uniform (`ARCHITECTURE/15` §5: "beat 'height' is proportional to narrative weight, not a uniform value repeated six times"). A page where every section gets the same padding and the same heading size regardless of what it's saying has hierarchy in name only.

**Editorial layouts and grid systems.** HubZero's layouts should read as composed, not templated — asymmetric where asymmetry serves the content (unequal panels, off-center emphasis), with a faint structural grid or dot-grid permitted as background texture where it reads as "engineered system" rather than decoration (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §3). The grid is infrastructure for composition, not a visible design feature in its own right; a grid line that exists to be seen and admired rather than to align content has become decoration wearing structure's clothes, the same failure category as motion without purpose.

**Photography.** HubZero shows real work and real people — real product screenshots, real team photography — never stock imagery standing in for either (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §7). This has a consistency requirement the site has not yet fully met: `docs/design-reviews/MARKETING_SITE_REVIEW_V1.md` §6 found the founder photos on the About page visibly inconsistent in lighting, background, and crop quality, "the exact section whose entire job is 'these are real, credible people you'd trust with six figures.'" Real photography is only a trust asset when it's _consistently_ real — a mismatched photo set reads as more amateur than a consistent, deliberately simple treatment would, because inconsistency itself is the tell a sophisticated visitor notices first, not photo quality in isolation.

**Illustration policy.** HubZero does not use illustration as a substitute for a real artifact. Where a diagram is warranted (a system architecture, a data flow, a process), it should be built as an actual technical diagram in the site's own type system — the Labs/R&D data-flow diagram is the reference case (§2 above) — rather than commissioned or generated illustration standing in for content the team hasn't actually built yet. If there's nothing real to diagram, that's information (the work isn't far enough along to show), not a gap to paper over with illustration.

**Motion philosophy.** Covered in depth in §2 ("motion with purpose") and §3.2. In design-language terms: motion is reserved for (a) one orchestrated load sequence per page, (b) brief entry reveals that support reading order, and (c) motion with an actual informational job (a metric counting up once, a filter transition). Every use of motion should survive being asked, out loud, "what does this animation teach the viewer that a static frame wouldn't?"

**Color usage.** A near-monochrome base (background, surface, border, text, muted-text) plus exactly one semantic accent color, used only for the primary CTA, active/focused states, and the brand mark (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §2). The signature gradient exists as one named token, used in one or two places per page, never as ambient background color. The discipline generalizes past the current token values: HubZero uses color to mean something (this is interactive, this is active, this is the one path forward) — never as a mood-setting device layered on for atmosphere.

**Spacing.** Generous section padding (96–128px desktop) as a floor, not a target to shrink toward — one primary idea per section, with room for it to breathe (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §3). Spacing communicates confidence: a page willing to leave room empty is a page that trusts its content doesn't need to be crammed to feel substantial.

**Responsiveness.** Every principle above holds at every viewport — restraint, hierarchy, and the uniqueness test apply as much to a 375px-wide screen as a 1440px one. A mobile layout is not a compressed afterthought; it's the same editorial argument, re-composed for a narrower canvas. See `ARCHITECTURE/16_RESPONSIVE_DESIGN_STANDARDS.md` for the concrete breakpoint and behavior rules this principle resolves into.

## 5. Emotional direction

HubZero's pages should leave a visitor feeling **calm, confident, thoughtful, precise, credible, and intentional.** These are not six independent moods to sprinkle across a page — they form one coherent emotional register, and it's worth being explicit about why each one matters and how they reinforce each other.

**Calm.** Nothing on a HubZero page should feel like it's competing for attention against the reader's own reading pace — no urgency banners, no countdown, no autoplay motion pulling focus mid-sentence. Calm is a trust signal specifically because urgency is the emotional tell of a company that isn't confident its offer stands on its own merits. A visitor evaluating a six-figure engagement is, correctly, suspicious of anything that feels like it's trying to rush a decision.

**Confident.** Confidence shows up as restraint (§2, "premium through restraint") and as directness (`02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md`'s hero register: state plainly what HubZero does, not a clever slogan hedging around it). A confident page doesn't need three different devices reassuring the reader in the same beat; it says the true thing once, clearly, and moves on.

**Thoughtful.** Every beat exists because it answers a question the previous beat raised (§2, "editorial instead of agency"). Thoughtfulness is legible in sequencing — a reader should feel like the page anticipated their next question rather than reciting a fixed list of things the company wanted to say regardless of order.

**Precise.** Precision is what specific, named, checkable facts feel like next to vague claims — "12 Mongoose data models" instead of "robust backend," a dated Labs project instead of an unattributed "we're always innovating." Typography reinforces this: tight tracking on display type, disciplined weight usage, and Geist Mono reserved for verifiable facts all _look_ precise before a reader has processed a single word, which matters because first impressions form faster than reading comprehension does.

**Credible.** Credibility is earned cumulatively across a page, not asserted once in a hero. `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` §7's trust-building sequence — claim, then honest small facts, then named capability, then hard evidence, then risk reduction, then the ask — is the template for how credibility should accumulate on any page, not just the homepage. A page that puts its hardest evidence first and its vaguest claims last has the sequence backwards.

**Intentional.** Every decision — a texture, a pause, a motif appearance — should read as chosen, not defaulted into. This is the emotional payoff of the uniqueness test (§6, §8): a page that passes it doesn't just avoid looking like a competitor's site, it _feels_ like someone made a deliberate call about every element in it, which is itself a form of the "engineering-first" credibility HubZero is trying to establish. Sloppy, unconsidered, or auto-generated-feeling design is the single fastest way to undercut a claim of engineering rigor, regardless of how rigorous the actual engineering is.

**Why this register and not another.** A playful, high-energy, "fun startup" register would contradict `02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §1's finding that the team wants to read as "a credible, scaled engineering/IT services company that happens to have excellent design taste" — not a consumer app. An austere, cold, minimalist-to-the-point-of-sterile register would undercut "credible" (sterile reads as impersonal, and HubZero's actual differentiator is a small, accountable, named team, not a faceless system). The calm-confident-precise register is the specific point on that spectrum that supports a founder-led engineering studio asking a skeptical buyer to trust it with real money.

## 6. Composition principles

**Every page earns its own composition** (§2) is a principle, not yet a method. This section is the method: for each part of the site, what is the composition _for_ — what question is it uniquely positioned to answer, and what would make its shape genuinely different from its neighbors, not just differently worded. Deliberately, this section does not prescribe layouts (`06_PAGE_SPECIFICATIONS.md`, `15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md`, and future page specs own that); it prescribes purpose, because purpose is what a designer or an AI system needs before layout decisions can be made well, and it's the exact thing that went missing between the homepage and the pages built after it.

**Home.** The only page whose job is to compress HubZero's entire pitch into one continuous read for a visitor who has given the site fifteen seconds and no prior context. Its composition should feel like an _opening statement_ — it establishes the claim, grounds it in honest facts, demonstrates it once with real evidence, and closes the loop. Nothing else on the site should attempt to be this compressed or this comprehensive; a services page trying to also be a mini-homepage is a composition mistake, not a content one.

**Work.** The evidence room. Its job is to let a skeptical evaluator verify HubZero has actually delivered, not to persuade — by the time someone is browsing individual case studies, persuasion's job is mostly done and proof's job has started. Composition should favor real specificity (stack, timeline, measured outcomes) over curation theater (elaborate case-study "reveal" animations that delay getting to the facts). As the second and third real case studies ship, Work's composition should resist becoming a uniform card grid where every entry looks interchangeable — the whole point of a small, selective portfolio is that each entry is worth reading individually, not scanning past.

**Builds.** Per `ARCHITECTURE/17_COMPANY_STRUCTURE.md` §2, Builds is evidence that HubZero's own exploration turns into something real enough to stand on its own — no client, fully owned. Its composition should communicate ownership and craft pride distinctly from Work's client-delivery framing: where Work's narrative is "here's what we solved for someone else," Builds' narrative is "here's what we decided was worth building, and why." A Builds detail page that reads identically to a Work case study (problem → approach → result → CTA) has missed the one thing that makes a Build interesting — that nobody commissioned it.

**Labs.** Evidence that HubZero keeps building when no one's watching (`17` §1). Its composition should be honest about incompleteness in a way none of the other pillars are — in-progress, exploratory, disclosed as not guaranteed to ship (`17` §2). This should be visible in the _design_, not just the copy: Labs entries can look more like a working notebook than a finished feature page, because a polished, fully "closed" composition on unfinished work would itself be a small dishonesty (§2, "never fabricate credibility," extended to visual finish level implying a false sense of completeness).

**Blueprints.** Evidence that HubZero's engineering discipline is repeatable, not a one-off performance (`17` §2). Its composition carries a burden the other three pillars don't: it must avoid looking so generic it undermines its own "not a template" claim (`17` §2's explicit warning). This means Blueprints' composition should foreground the specific, lived provenance of each foundation — what real engagement or Labs work it was distilled from, what decisions it embodies — rather than presenting as an interchangeable theme-marketplace listing. A live demo link and honest `demoStatus` disclosure (`17` §4) are part of the composition, not an implementation afterthought; a stale demo is a visible credibility failure the moment a visitor clicks it.

**Services.** The page for a visitor asking "can you actually do the specific thing I need?" Its composition should feel like a _capability reference_, not a repeat of the homepage's argument — the homepage already established trust in general; Services' job is to let a visitor confirm their specific need is covered, quickly, without re-litigating whether HubZero is credible at all. This is precisely where the shipped site's failure occurred (`docs/design-reviews/MARKETING_SITE_REVIEW_V1.md` §3: Services' hero headline repeats `WhatWeDo`'s homepage line verbatim, "a stall, not a build"). Services and its child pages (Software, Hardware & Embedded) should each answer a different sub-question with a different shape — not the same shape with the discipline name swapped.

**About.** The page for a visitor asking "who, specifically, am I trusting with this?" Its composition earns the right to be the most textual, least visually elaborate page on the site — a long-form read, closer to a well-written essay than an editorial spread (the shipped About page's own reasoning, per the review, correctly departs from the homepage's asymmetric-panel language in favor of straightforward prose sections). The one place About cannot compromise is photographic consistency (§4 above) — a page whose entire purpose is "these are real, credible people" is the single worst place on the site for visual polish to lapse.

**The check that applies to all of the above, every time:** before a new page in any of these families ships, apply the uniqueness test from `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` §12, generalized site-wide by `07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §8 — could this page's macro-composition appear on another agency's site by swapping the logo? Apply it per page, not once at the end of a batch (the mistake that produced the Services/Software/Hardware pattern), and apply it against the other pages already live on the site, not just against generic competitors — the more urgent question, by this point in HubZero's history, is "does this look like the last page I built," not just "does this look like a stranger's site."

## 7. AI prompting rules

These rules govern how design work should be _requested_, whether the requester is a human briefing an AI tool or one AI system briefing another. A bad prompt produces generic output even from a system that knows every rule in this document, because the rules only bind if the prompt invokes them — an AI system has no way to infer "avoid glassmorphism" from a bare request to "design a hero section."

1. **Always specify what to avoid, not only what to achieve.** A prompt that only states the goal ("a hero for the Builds page") gives a generative system nothing to differentiate HubZero's hero from the statistical average hero it was trained on. A prompt that adds "not a centered SaaS layout, not a browser-chrome product mockup, not the same asymmetric-serif-headline shape the homepage already used" gives it an actual constraint to design against. §3 of this document is the standing source list for this — cite it directly rather than re-deriving a fresh "don't" list each time.

2. **Never ask for "modern."** "Modern" has no stable referent — it means whatever visual convention is currently oversaturated, which is exactly the convention this document exists to avoid. If the underlying intent is "not dated, not clunky, not visually cheap," say that instead, because those are real, checkable qualities; "modern" is not.

3. **Never ask for "premium" without defining what premium means for this specific page.** "Premium" as a bare adjective produces gold gradients and serif logotypes — the aesthetic cliché of premium, not the substance of it. §2's actual definition of premium at HubZero ("restraint, specificity, evidence over assertion") is what should go in the prompt instead: "restrained, evidence-led, not visually loud" is an instruction a system can act on; "premium" is not.

4. **Always define success in terms of what a reader should be able to do or verify afterward**, not in terms of how the page should look. "A visitor should be able to tell, within the first screen, that this is exploratory and unfinished, not a shipped product" is a success criterion. "It should look clean and professional" is not — it's a description with no falsifiable failure condition.

5. **Always define the emotional goal explicitly, using §5's vocabulary, and say why that emotion fits this specific page.** "Calm and confident, because this is a case study and the reader should feel like they're reading real evidence, not being sold to" gives a generative system a register to aim for. An unstated emotional goal defaults to whatever register the system's training data associates with the literal words in the prompt — usually a generic "exciting/dynamic" startup register, which is wrong for nearly everything HubZero builds.

6. **Always define the interaction/motion philosophy for the specific piece being requested**, even if the answer is "no motion beyond a standard entry reveal." Leaving motion unspecified defaults to "add some animations to make it feel more premium" — precisely the failure §2 and §3.2 both name directly.

7. **Always reference HubZero's design principles by name**, not by re-explaining them from scratch each time. Cite §2's specific principle that's most load-bearing for this task ("this needs to pass 'every page earns its own composition' against the Services page specifically") rather than a generic "make it look good and on-brand." A named, specific principle is checkable; "on-brand" is not, especially for a system with no persistent memory of what "on-brand" has meant on prior tasks.

8. **Always optimize for comprehension before aesthetics**, and say so in the prompt if there's any risk of the two trading off. When a generative system has to choose between a more legible hierarchy and a more visually striking arrangement, an unstated preference will often resolve toward striking, because striking is easier to reward in a single-image evaluation than comprehension is. State the priority explicitly.

9. **State which page or pillar this design serves, and name at least one sibling page it must not resemble.** This is the direct countermeasure to the Services/Software/Hardware failure: "this is the Hardware & Embedded page; it must not share Software Engineering's beat order (caption → headline → mono facts → numbered panels → pull-quote → process list → CTA)" is a prompt that can actually prevent the failure the review found. A prompt with no sibling-awareness has no way to avoid recreating it.

10. **Distinguish a request for a _component_ from a request for a _composition_, and say which one it is.** "Design a reusable card component for the filter index pages" and "design the Blueprints detail page" require opposite instincts — the first should be as reusable and consistent as possible; the second should actively resist reusing another page's macro-structure. Conflating the two in a single prompt (as most generic "design a page" requests implicitly do) is how a component-reuse instinct quietly becomes a composition-reuse mistake.

11. **When iterating on existing HubZero work, name the specific device being reused and how many times it's already appeared.** Before asking for "another italic pull-quote here," check §3.4's budget logic and say so in the prompt: "this would be the pull-quote's fourth appearance across the site — either give it a new job or don't use it here." This is the operational fix for the overuse failures §3.4 documents; it has to happen at prompt time, because it's much harder to catch after the output already looks polished.

12. **Ask for the memorable moment before asking for the layout.** Per `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` §8a's method: name the one or two specific compositional decisions a reader should recall tomorrow, before specifying the rest of the page's structure. A layout built outward from "what should someone remember" produces a genuinely different result than a layout built from "what sections does this page need," even when the two end up covering the same content.

## 8. Design review checklist

Run this checklist before any design output — human or AI-generated — is considered done. It is written as questions with teeth: each one names the specific failure it's checking for, drawn from principles and real incidents documented above, not a generic "does this look good" pass.

**Genericness and template risk**

- Does this resemble a generic SaaS/agency template — could another company swap its logo onto this and lose nothing? (§2, "every decision should support trust"; §1's founding incident.)
- Have you checked this page's macro-composition against the pages that will sit next to it in a real user journey, not just against generic competitors? (§6's closing note; this is the specific check the Services/Software/Hardware failure skipped.)
- If this is the second, third, or later page in a family (a pillar's detail pages, a set of service pages), does it share a beat order, component set, and transition style with an earlier page in that family — and if so, is that a deliberately reused _component_, or an accidentally reused _composition_? (§1, §6, `07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §8.)

**Composition and structural honesty**

- Does every section justify its existence — could it be deleted, reordered, or duplicated with no loss of meaning? If so, it was built as a swappable agency block, not an editorial beat. (§2, "editorial instead of agency.")
- Does each beat hand off to the next with intent, or could the page be read as a stack of independent sections in any order? (`ARCHITECTURE/15` §2's narrative arc requirement.)
- If the page removed all decoration — texture, motif, gradient, imagery — would the typographic composition alone still read as confident and deliberate? (§2, "typography before decoration.")

**Device budget and overuse**

- Is this the Nth use of a HubZero-specific device (serif pull-quote, whitespace-climax pause, circuit motif, a specific process framing) across the site, and does this use add a new job for it to do, or is it coasting on the device's earlier credibility? (§3.4 — the most likely place for a technically-compliant design to still fail.)
- Is the signature accent color, gradient token, or motif being used at or below its documented budget (one accent color for CTA/active states; gradient in one or two places per page; circuit motif at its scoped appearance count)? (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §2, `15` §1.)

**Motion**

- Does every motion effect answer "what does this teach the viewer that a static frame wouldn't?" — and if asked afterward what animated on the page, would an honest reader say "I'm not sure, I was reading"? (§2, `15` §8.)
- Is `prefers-reduced-motion` respected everywhere motion is used? (`07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §4, §6.)

**Typography and hierarchy**

- Is typography doing enough work on its own — would a stripped-down, decoration-free version of this page still communicate the correct hierarchy and emphasis? (§2, §4.)
- Is the display serif (or any typeface reserved for rare moments) used only where the reader should genuinely slow down, not as a routine section-opener? (§3.4.)
- Does heading structure (`h1`→`h2`→`h3`) correctly reflect the page's actual information hierarchy, independent of visual styling — a screen-reader user navigating by heading level should get the same structure a sighted user perceives visually? (`docs/design-reviews/MARKETING_SITE_REVIEW_V1.md` §7's real finding on this.)

**Trust and credibility**

- Does the page communicate trust through specific, checkable facts rather than adjectives or round numbers? (§2, "never fabricate credibility.") Is any number on the page one a skeptical visitor could actually verify, and would it survive being checked?
- Is anything presented with a finish level (polish, completeness, production-grade visual treatment) that overstates how finished the underlying work actually is — particularly on Labs or Builds entries, where no client is present to catch the overstatement? (`ARCHITECTURE/17_COMPANY_STRUCTURE.md` §6.)
- Is photography (team, product) consistent in lighting, background, and crop quality across every instance on the page, especially anywhere the page's job is to establish that real, credible people are behind the work? (§4; the About-page finding.)

**Decoration and noise**

- Is anything on the page decorative without a specific purpose — an icon, a texture, a gradient, or a motif element that could be removed with no loss of meaning? (§2, "design should improve comprehension"; §3.2.)
- Would this page, read cold with no context, be mistaken for a stock template by someone who has seen a hundred agency sites? (§1's founding test, restated as a final gate.)

**Close with the founder-level gate**, carried forward unchanged from `ARCHITECTURE/15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md` §12 because it has already been validated as the right bar:

- Does this still feel editorial rather than SaaS?
- Does this still feel timeless rather than trendy?
- Does this still feel premium rather than flashy?
- Does this still feel like one uninterrupted narrative rather than stacked sections?
- Would a potential client remember this experience tomorrow?
- Is every animation earning its place?
- Is every design decision helping build trust?
- Is this recognizably HubZero rather than recognizably AI-generated?

A design output that passes every item above except the last one has still failed. "Recognizably HubZero" is not a vibe check — it means a reader who has seen two or three other HubZero pages would recognize the type discipline, the restraint, and the evidence-first structure, while still being able to tell this page apart from those others by its composition. Passing every rule while still looking like this document was never consulted is the specific failure this whole checklist exists to catch.

## 9. Long-term evolution

HubZero's design language will change over the coming years — the company will have more case studies, a growing Builds catalog, real Blueprint demos, more team photography, possibly a different accent color or typeface if the brand itself evolves. This section separates what should hold through all of that from what's free to change, so a future revision of the site (or of this document) can tell the difference between updating an implementation detail and abandoning a founding principle.

**What should remain stable.** The nine principles in §2 are the load-bearing structure — editorial over agency, engineering-first over marketing-first, restraint as the signal of premium, purposeful motion, per-page compositional earning, comprehension over aesthetics, zero fabrication, and trust as the test every decision answers to. These principles are not tied to any specific typeface, color value, or component; they are why HubZero rejected the legacy site's blur orbs and GSAP-everywhere in the first place (`ARCHITECTURE/02_BRAND_STRATEGY.md` §3), and they should outlive several complete visual refreshes. The uniqueness test (§6, §8) should also remain a permanent, standing gate — it is a _process_ guarantee, not a specific rule about any one page, and the one incident this document was written in direct response to (§1) is exactly what happens when that gate exists on paper but isn't actually run per page.

**What may evolve.** Specific tokens — the exact type scale, the specific gradient hex values, the circuit-trace motif itself, the current accent blue — are all implementation choices made once, against the company's needs and maturity at a specific point in time (`ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md`, `15_HOMEPAGE_DESIGN.md`). As HubZero grows — more engineers, more disciplines, more shipped work — the specific visual signature is allowed to grow with it, the same way a magazine's typography and photography style shifts across a decade of otherwise-consistent editorial values. A future team should feel free to retire the circuit motif, change the accent color, or adopt a new display typeface, provided the _reasoning_ behind whatever replaces them can pass the same tests this document lays out — restraint, purpose, per-page earning, comprehension, trust. What should never be permitted is a visual refresh justified by "this looks more current" alone — that is precisely the "never ask for modern" failure (§7) applied to the design system itself, at a larger scale.

**How this document should change.** Treat a proposed change to this document with the same rigor `ARCHITECTURE/17_COMPANY_STRUCTURE.md` §7 applies to proposing a fifth company pillar: a change is justified only if an existing principle genuinely fails to cover the new situation, not merely because a new trend makes an old principle feel inconvenient. Most future design questions should resolve as an _application_ of an existing principle in this document to a new page or medium, not as grounds for a new principle. When a real, durable change is needed — a new medium (e.g., a dedicated design system for a future product UI, distinct from the marketing site), a genuinely new pillar's compositional needs, or a principle proven wrong by a real incident the way the Services/Software/Hardware pattern proved the uniqueness test needed to be a standing rule rather than a homepage-only footnote — log it explicitly, the way `ARCHITECTURE/18_ARCHITECTURE_CHANGELOG.md` logs architectural revisions, so a future reader can tell _why_ the guide changed, not just that it did.

**The test that outlives every specific rule in this document:** as HubZero accumulates more real work — more case studies, more Builds, more Labs entries, more Blueprints — the temptation will grow to lean on volume and polish instead of on the restraint and specificity that got the company here. Resist it. A larger, more established HubZero has _more_ evidence to be specific and honest with, not less need for the discipline. The day this document's principles start feeling like they're holding the design back rather than sharpening it is the day to check whether the company has genuinely outgrown a principle — or whether the shortcut being considered is the exact generic convenience this document exists to keep out.
