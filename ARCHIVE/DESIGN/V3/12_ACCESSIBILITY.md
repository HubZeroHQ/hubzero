# 12 — Accessibility

> **Status: New foundational document — 2026-07-11.** `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §6 established v2's accessibility baseline (WCAG 2.1 AA contrast, visible focus states, meaningful alt text, labeled forms, `prefers-reduced-motion`) and nothing in it is retired. This document does two things v2's single paragraph couldn't: it gathers accessibility into one owned place the way `CRITIQUE_01_REVIEW_BOARD.md` Part 8 found missing, and it extends the baseline to cover exactly the surfaces Working Blueprint adds that v2 never had — thin hairline borders, low-opacity linework, a second signal color, and diagram-as-primary-content. A system that leans this hard into thin lines, muted tones, and small mono labels needs a dedicated accessibility document more than most, not less.

## 0. Standing principle

Every rule below answers to one test, extended from `01_VISION.md` §3's "would a reader remember this tomorrow" standard: **would this page still work for a reader who can't see the accent color, can't perceive fine contrast differences, can't use a mouse, or has motion sensitivity?** If the honest answer is no, the design isn't finished — regardless of how well it satisfies the visual language in `02`–`08`. Accessibility is not a separate compliance pass applied after a design is settled; it's one of the same standing gates `09_PAGE_ARCHETYPES.md` §13 already applies per page, alongside the Uniqueness Test and the Recognition Test.

## 1. Contrast

Carried forward from `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §6, restated with the specific new pairs Working Blueprint introduces:

- **WCAG 2.1 AA minimum (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text and UI component boundaries) on every real text/background and stroke/background pairing** — this now explicitly includes pairs v2 never had to test: signal-gold text or icons against both solder-mask dark and drafting-paper light, drafting-cyan linework against both bases, and every hairline border (`06_COMPONENT_LANGUAGE.md`'s tags, callouts, table dividers) against its surrounding surface.
- **Hairline borders are a new legibility risk v2 didn't have and must be tested as one.** `06_COMPONENT_LANGUAGE.md` §2, §7, §9 replace filled cards and pill backgrounds with thin 1px hairline rules — a real, load-bearing design decision, not a decorative one, which means its actual contrast ratio against the surface it sits on must clear 3:1 (WCAG's non-text contrast minimum) at every theme and every zoom level, not just "look fine at 100% on a reference monitor." A hairline that passes at 100% zoom and fails at 150% (a real, common low-vision browsing pattern) has not passed this requirement.
- **Low-opacity linework (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §11's section-cut cross-hatching, any dot-grid texture) is exempted from the 3:1 requirement only because it is explicitly decorative and non-informational** — the moment any hairline or texture starts carrying real information (a diagram's actual connective line, a table's actual row divider), it graduates to the 3:1 requirement above. A designer or AI system must be able to answer, for any thin line on a page, "is this decorative or informational" before deciding which bar it needs to clear.
- **Numerals and technical labels (`03_TYPOGRAPHY.md` §7, §9) are frequently set small (13–14px) and in Geist Mono** — small monospace type has less inherent legibility margin than a humanist sans at the same size, so caption-scale mono text is held to the higher end of AA's large-text exemption reasoning, not the lower end: verify actual measured contrast, never assume mono "reads fine" at a size that would fail if it were prose.

## 2. Color-vision deficiency

This is the single biggest accessibility gap `CRITIQUE_01_REVIEW_BOARD.md` found in the original color system, and it is treated here as a gating requirement, not an aside — even though `11_COLOR_PHILOSOPHY_AMENDMENT.md` §5 notes that a blue-family palette starts from a genuinely safer position than a warm orange/amber accent would have, since it sits far from the red–green confusion axis that affects protanopia and deuteranopia, the two most common CVD forms. That starting advantage is a reason to expect this audit to go well — it is not a substitute for running it.

- **Before any of `11_COLOR_PHILOSOPHY_AMENDMENT.md` §2's seven stops are treated as final, each must be simulated against protanopia, deuteranopia, and tritanopia** (the three most common CVD forms) using a real simulation tool (e.g. a Coblis- or Stark-class simulator) at the exact hex values chosen, not at a generic swatch — with particular attention to Brand Blue and Electric Sky against Structural Blue (the two closest-in-value pairs on the ramp, and the ones most likely to collapse into each other under tritanopia specifically, the one CVD form blue-family hues are not inherently safer against).
- **The pass criterion is functional, not cosmetic:** a reader with any of the three common CVD forms must still be able to (a) tell that Brand Blue text or a Brand Blue button is distinct from surrounding body text and from status colors (danger/warning/success/info), and (b) tell that Ice Blue linework (`11_COLOR_PHILOSOPHY_AMENDMENT.md` §6's technical-diagram register) is distinct from the neutral ink used for ordinary diagram labels. If either fails under simulation, the specific lightness/chroma value — not the palette's underlying hue family — should be adjusted until it passes, before the token is finalized in `10_IMPLEMENTATION_ROADMAP.md` Phase 1.
- **Color is never the only signal of state, full stop** — carried forward from `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §6 and made concrete for every new component in `06_COMPONENT_LANGUAGE.md`: an active tag (§7) carries the Brand Blue underline _and_ a visible weight or icon change, never color alone; a form error carries an icon and text _and_ the danger hue, never a red border alone; a diagram's Ice Blue linework is always paired with a real label or legend, never asked to communicate "this is the technical layer" through hue alone to a reader who can't perceive it as distinct from ink.
- **Status colors** (danger/warning/success/info, `04_COLOR.md` §4) must be simulated the same way and must remain distinguishable from every stop in `11_COLOR_PHILOSOPHY_AMENDMENT.md` §2's palette and from each other under all three CVD forms — this is a new, explicit requirement `04_COLOR.md` named as a category to re-tune but never specified a test for.

## 3. Diagrams and technical drawings

Diagrams are, per `01_VISION.md` §7 and `02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §9, the identity's single most important device — which makes them the single highest-stakes accessibility surface on the site, not an exception to it.

- **Every diagram must be legible with all color removed.** Per `02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §9's own convention (real schematic symbols, real box/arrow/queue convention), a diagram's structure must already be readable from shape, line weight, and label alone — Ice Blue marks "this is a technical drawing" as a register, it must never be the only thing distinguishing one element from another within the drawing (two connected nodes, two different signal paths). This is a direct extension of `02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §10's existing annotation-optionality rule ("a reader who ignores every annotation should still understand the diagram") to color specifically.
- **Every diagram needs a real text alternative, not a generic `alt="diagram"`.** A site-native diagram built from the site's own SVG/type tokens (`06_COMPONENT_LANGUAGE.md` §15) must carry either a structured text equivalent (a `<figcaption>` that states what the diagram shows and its key relationships, per `02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §5's figure-caption convention already doing most of this work) or, for a genuinely complex system diagram, a linked plain-text/table equivalent nearby — never an empty or purely decorative `alt`, and never `alt` text that just repeats the figure's own caption verbatim without adding the structural information a sighted reader gets from the drawing itself.
- **Annotations (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §10) must be reachable by keyboard and screen reader when interactive**, not only by hover — a leader-line callout that only reveals its detail on `:hover` fails a keyboard-only or screen-reader user outright. Where an annotation is interactive (a tooltip, an expandable detail), it must be a real, focusable, `aria`-labeled control (§5 below), not a hover-only affordance.
- **GSAP-driven architecture-diagram sequencing (`08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §3) must have a fully legible static end-state that requires no animation to understand** — already a stated principle in `08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §6 principle 4 ("motion is additive, never load-bearing for comprehension"); this document makes the accessibility consequence explicit: a diagram whose build-order animation is skipped (via `prefers-reduced-motion`, a slow connection, or a screen reader) must render its fully-resolved, fully-connected state immediately, not a partially-drawn one.

## 4. Motion and vestibular safety

Extends `08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §6 principle 3 with the specific thresholds that principle referenced but didn't state:

- **`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` means an instantly resolved static state, not a shorter or slower version of the same animation** — restated from `08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §6 principle 3 because it's the single most commonly under-implemented reduced-motion rule industry-wide (a "shorter fade" still moves, and still triggers vestibular discomfort for the population this setting exists to protect).
- **No animation may exceed three flashes per second at any point**, a hard line against seizure risk that applies most directly to Anime.js's SVG-drawing and counter animations (`08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §5) — a trace-path draw-in or a rapidly-incrementing counter must be checked against this threshold explicitly if its default easing would otherwise produce a strobing effect at small sizes.
- **GSAP pinned/scroll-scrubbed sequences (`08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §3) are a real vestibular-disorder risk category (scroll-linked motion that moves content independently of the reader's own scroll input) and must degrade to a normal, unpinned static layout under `prefers-reduced-motion`** — not a slower pin, a fully unpinned page. This is the same standard `08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §6 principle 3 already sets for GSAP via `gsap.matchMedia()`; this document names the specific failure mode (residual pinning) that a partial implementation could otherwise miss.
- **Parallax, cursor-following effects, and gratuitous 3D transforms are already excluded outright** (`08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §8) for comprehension reasons; this document adds that all three are also disproportionately likely to trigger motion sensitivity, which is a second, independent reason none of them should ever be reconsidered as a "tasteful exception."

## 5. Keyboard navigation and focus

- **Every interactive element has a visible focus state, carried forward unmodified from `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §6** — under Working Blueprint's component language, this now explicitly includes the new hairline-bordered tags (`06_COMPONENT_LANGUAGE.md` §7), the custom trace-geometry icon buttons (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §12), and any interactive diagram control (§3 above). A focus ring drawn only in Ice Blue or only as a subtle border-color shift is not sufficient on its own — verify it independently clears the 3:1 non-text contrast minimum (§1) against every surface it can appear on, light and dark.
- **Tab order follows the drafting-sheet reading order** (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §1's title-block-then-body structure), not raw DOM order left to accident — a page whose visual hierarchy reads top-to-bottom, title-block-then-content must have a tab order that matches, especially on asymmetric-panel layouts (`05_LAYOUT_SYSTEM.md` §5) where the DOM's default order and the visual reading order can easily diverge.
- **Custom icons (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §12) carry `aria-label` or equivalent accessible names wherever they're interactive**, and are marked `aria-hidden` wherever they're purely decorative next to a text label that already states the same thing — the same rule `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §6 already states for icon-only buttons, extended to the new custom trace-geometry icon vocabulary specifically because it has no third-party library defaults to inherit accessible naming from; every icon in the future icon reference sheet (`14_VISUAL_TOKENS.md` §6) must ship with its accessible name specified alongside its visual spec, not left for an implementer to guess.
- **The mobile nav drawer (`06_COMPONENT_LANGUAGE.md` §3) traps focus correctly while open and returns focus to its trigger on close** — an existing, unremarkable requirement restated here because Working Blueprint's migration of the drawer's open/close to Motion (`08_MOTION_SYSTEM.md` §4) is exactly the kind of implementation change that can silently regress focus management if it isn't explicitly retested after the migration.
- **Skip-to-content remains available** on every page, unaffected by the drafting-sheet title-block pattern (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §1) that now precedes most page content — a title block is a visual/informational device, not a navigation obstacle, and must not require a keyboard user to tab through it to reach the page's real content.

## 6. Forms and semantic structure

- **Every form field has a programmatically associated label, and every error is announced, not just shown** — carried forward unmodified from `ARCHITECTURE/07_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` §6. The Contact page (`09_PAGE_ARCHETYPES.md` §12), as the site's one conversion-critical form, is the highest-stakes single surface for this rule; a successful submission's confirmation state (already flagged as under-specified by `CRITIQUE_01_REVIEW_BOARD.md` Part 5) must be announced to assistive technology (a live region or focus-moved confirmation), not conveyed only through a visual text swap.
- **Heading hierarchy is real and sequential**, not chosen for visual scale alone — a `03_TYPOGRAPHY.md` §2 display headline is not automatically an `<h1>` if the drafting-sheet title block above it (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §1) already carries the page's real `<h1>`; every page's heading levels must nest correctly regardless of which element happens to be styled largest.
- **Figure captions (`02_VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md` §5) use real `<figure>`/`<figcaption>` markup**, not a styled `<div>` pair, so a screen reader user gets the same "this is a captioned figure" structural signal a sighted reader gets from the visual convention alone.

## 7. What this document does not do

It does not specify exact contrast values, exact CVD-simulation output, or a finished audit — those are implementation-phase verification work, one level below what a system document commits to, exactly as `10_IMPLEMENTATION_ROADMAP.md` Phase 1 already scopes a "real WCAG AA contrast audit" as part of the token migration. This document's job is to make sure that audit actually happens, against the right pairs, against the right CVD forms, and against the right new surfaces Working Blueprint introduces — not to pre-compute its results.

`10_IMPLEMENTATION_ROADMAP.md` Phase 1's effort estimate is amended by this document only in scope, not sequencing: the contrast and CVD audits in §1–2 above are additions to Phase 1's existing "real WCAG AA contrast audit" line item, not a new phase.
