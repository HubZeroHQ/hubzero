# Product Launches SEO

Launch content — a new product, a major feature, a new AI capability — trades the long, compounding discovery curve of evergreen content for a short window where timeliness, clarity, and a single unambiguous narrative matter most.

---

# Search Intent

At launch, visitors typically search to:

- Confirm the announcement is real and understand what changed
- Understand whether it applies to them
- Find out how to access or try it
- Compare it to what existed before, or to competitors

---

# Primary Keyword Categories

- Product or feature name, exactly as announced
- "What is [product/feature]"
- Comparisons ("[product] vs [alternative]")
- Availability and access terms ("[product] pricing", "[product] waitlist", "[product] release date")

---

# Essential Pages

Required

- A single canonical launch page or post
- Updated product/feature pages reflecting the new capability
- Updated navigation and pricing pages if the launch changes them

Recommended

- A comparison or migration page if the launch replaces something
- Press or media resources, if applicable

---

# Information Architecture

Every launch should have exactly one canonical URL that the announcement, social posts, and press all point to. Avoid splitting the narrative across multiple competing pages that all rank for the same query.

Once the launch window passes, fold durable information (what the feature does, how to use it) into evergreen documentation or product pages, and let the launch page itself age into an archived announcement rather than the primary source of truth.

---

# Internal Linking Strategy

Link the launch page to the permanent product or feature page it describes, and vice versa, from the moment both exist. Update older, related pages to reference the new capability rather than leaving them describing a now-outdated state of the product.

---

# Structured Data

Recommended

- Article or NewsArticle for the announcement
- Product, if the launch introduces a purchasable or distinct product surface

---

# Content Strategy

Lead with what changed and why it matters to the reader, not with internal project history. State availability (who, when, how) unambiguously — ambiguity here is the most common cause of avoidable support load right after a launch.

---

# Technical Considerations

Ensure the launch page is indexable and crawlable before the announcement goes out, not after. Confirm Open Graph and social card metadata specifically — launch content is disproportionately shared on social platforms.

---

# Common Mistakes

- Multiple pages competing for the same launch query
- Launch page left as the only source of truth long after the feature is no longer new
- Ambiguous availability information
- Missing or incorrect social share metadata at the moment of announcement

---

# Success Metrics

Time-to-index after publish, share and referral traffic in the launch window, conversion from launch page to trial/adoption, query volume for the product or feature name over time.
